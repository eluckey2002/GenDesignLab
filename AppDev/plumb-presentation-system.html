02 / The problem

A grammar between brand and finished slide.

Most design systems handle two layers: how the brand looks (color, type, logo) and what slide layouts exist (templates). Both are necessary. Neither makes a slide actually communicate.

The missing layer is the one between them — a system that defines how information behaves. What role each piece is playing in the story. What visual primitive expresses that role. How those primitives compose into reusable components. How the components flex through variants and states without becoming rigid templates.

This document defines that layer. It sits inside the brand system and powers the catalog above it. Slide-type catalog is downstream output, not foundation.

A slide can hold a great deal of information when the information is structured well. The discipline is structure, not subtraction.
03 / The science

Why the system works isn't aesthetics.

The components in this system don't perform because they look right. They perform because they map onto how perception, working memory, and reading actually function. Seven foundations the system rests on — cited where they earn the page in component definitions below.

03.1
Cognitive load theory
Sweller, 1988
Working memory holds about four chunks at a time. Slides that exceed it force re-reading or refusal. Structure (hierarchy, grouping, recurring shapes) reduces extraneous load and lets the audience spend its bandwidth on the content, not the parsing.
Used by every component below. Especially: hierarchy in metric treatments, grouping in caveat rails, schemas across the system.
03.2
Pre-attentive processing
Treisman, 1985 · Few, 2009
A small set of visual properties — color, position, length, orientation, shape — are detected by the eye in under 250 ms, before conscious attention engages. The reader sees them before they read.
Risk markers (color), status ribbons (color + shape), milestone nodes (orientation), KPI position-encoding.
03.3
Gestalt grouping
Wertheimer, 1923 · Koffka, 1935
The brain auto-groups by proximity, similarity, continuity, closure. Visual hierarchy isn't optional — the eye will impose one whether you design it or not. The job is to design the one you want.
Caveat rail proximity, status ribbon similarity, hairline bridge continuity, every divider variant.
03.4
Encoding hierarchy
Cleveland & McGill, JASA 1984
For perceptual accuracy: position on a common scale > length > angle > area > color saturation. Pick the encoding by the comparison the reader needs to make. Position is the only encoding the eye reads precisely.
Metric treatments (position), KPI rules (length), risk severity (color, used because saturation contrast not magnitude is what matters).
03.5
Data-ink & lie factor
Tufte, Visual Display 1983 · Beautiful Evidence 2006
Maximize the proportion of ink that shows data over ink that decorates it. A chart's lie factor — the ratio of effect shown to effect in the data — should be 1.0. Defaults aren't neutral; they will lie for you if you let them.
Restraint as system principle. Every gradient, every gridline, every divider has to earn its place. Truth gets its own implementation note.
03.6
Schema theory
Bartlett, 1932 · Rumelhart, 1980
Recurring structures build mental scaffolds. A reader who's seen the same status ribbon shape forty times parses the forty-first without effort. Schemas are bandwidth — and consistency is how a system earns them.
Why every component has a single canonical form. Why variants are constrained. Why the system insists on consistency of slot order.
03.7
Information foraging
Pirolli & Card, 1995
Readers scan for information scent — small cues that signal "what kind of thing this is" before reading content. Eyebrow labels, ribbons, KPI numerals, caveat chips: all scent markers. They tell the reader where to invest attention.
Eyebrow primitives, status ribbons, caveat chips, source rules, metric labels — every scent-bearing element in the system.
04 / Architecture

Six layers. Top to bottom.

Most design systems work the top and bottom — brand and templates — and skip the meaning layer where the work happens. This is the full stack.

01
Brand System
Look & feel
Color, typography, motion, voice. Plumb's Studio Mode lives here. The visual identity layer.
02
Visual Primitives
Reusable visual atoms
Flag, chip, rail, node, connector, badge, marker, label, annotation, divider, bracket, glyph, ribbon, source rule. Atoms with variants.
03
Narrative Primitives
Story roles
Claim · Takeaway · Evidence · Caveat · Implication · Tension · Contrast · Recommendation · Decision · Risk · Next Step · Honest Translation. Meaning roles, not visuals.
04

Primitives composed
A narrative primitive expressed by a visual primitive, with required and optional slots. Risk marker. Milestone node. Decision gate. Proof badge. Caveat chip. Metric treatment. Dependency connector.

Output, not foundation
The downstream artifact a team draws from in PowerPoint. Generated by the system above, not the source of truth. A team can build any slide type they need from layers 02–05.
05 / Layer 03 · Narrative primitives

Twelve roles. Every line in every slide is one of these.

A narrative primitive defines what a piece of information is doing in the story. Before "how should this look," ask "what role is it playing." Visual choices follow narrative roles, never the reverse.

05.1
Claim
Job · the assertion the slide rests on
The main point the audience must understand. The thing you'd say if you only had one sentence.
"Multi-model reduces failure-mode exposure by 40%."
05.2
Takeaway
Job · the line they remember a week later
The distilled point — often shorter and sharper than the claim. The line you want quoted.
"Orchestration is the differentiator now. Speed isn't."
05.3
Evidence
Job · proof under the claim
Quantitative or qualitative support — sourced, dated, sized. The thing that earns the claim.
"Eval pass-rate climbed 23 pts; n=2,400 across four windows."
05.4
Caveat
Job · the boundary on the claim
A nuance, condition, or scope that prevents oversimplification. Trust accelerator, not hedge.
"Holds while context windows stay under 8K tokens."
05.5
Implication
Job · the so-what
What the information means for the next decision, the strategy, or the operator's day.
"Priority shifts from feature expansion to workflow reliability."
05.6
Tension
Job · the conflict that makes the story matter
The unresolved problem. Without it, slides are recitation. With it, they have a reason to exist.
"Teams want flexibility; leadership needs control. Both can't win."
05.7
Contrast
Job · two states held against each other
A structured comparison — A vs. B, before vs. after, current vs. future. The delta is the point.
"40 min per ticket → 12 min per ticket."
05.8
Recommendation
Job · the proposed action
The path Plumb's voice picks and defends. Hedged recommendations read as no recommendation.
"Adopt multi-model with one default and two fallbacks."
05.9
Decision
Job · the choice awaiting an answer
A specific question that needs a verdict. Distinct from a recommendation: a question, not an answer.
"Standardize on one model — or support multiple?"
05.10
Risk
Job · a caveat with consequence
A surfaced threat with severity and (ideally) mitigation. Distinct from a caveat — a caveat bounds; a risk warns.
"High · token-cost differential could exceed 1.4× through Q3."
05.11
Next Step
Job · the concrete action that follows
Owner, deadline, definition of done. The slide's discharge into reality.
"Align on design-system scope by Wk 4. Owner: e.luckey."
05.12
Honest Translation
Job · ground polished claim in plain English
Plumb's quiet refusal to sound smarter than it is. Polished claim, hairline rule, plain version.
"…Plainly: if your team can't run it without us, we didn't finish."
06 / Layer 02 · Visual primitives

Fourteen atoms. Every component below is built from these.

A visual primitive is a small reusable element with a single visual job. Each has variants. Components are made by combining primitives — not by drawing new shapes.

06.1 / Flag
Flag
A small directional shape that points at content or status.
variants · solid, hollow, severity-coded
06.2 / Chip
Chip
A bordered label with a single short string. Distinct from a ribbon: no trailing slash.
variants · neutral, scoped, bound, tone-coded
scoped
06.3 / Rail
Rail
A vertical hairline that organizes flush-left content; tick marks anchor sections.
variants · 2-mark, 3-mark, full-bleed
06.4 / Node
Node
A rotated diamond marking a discrete point. Filled / hollow encodes state.
variants · future, current, past, blocked
06.5 / Connector
Connector
A Bronze hairline punctuated by node joints. Replaces arrows in flow diagrams.
variants · linear, fan-out, fan-in, conditional
06.6 / Badge
Badge

variants · verification, confidence, ownership

Marker

variants · dot, diamond, glyph, numeric

07 / Layer 04 · Component recipes

Seven recipes. Each one a narrative role, expressed by a visual primitive, with slots that flex.

A component recipe combines layer 02 (visual) with layer 03 (narrative) into a reusable element with a clear job. Each entry below documents communication job, narrative primitive, visual primitive, anatomy, slots (required + optional), variants and states, example, and failure mode. Same eight fields, every time. Schemas earn bandwidth.

08 / Layer 05 · Composition patterns

Eight communication jobs. Not slide types.

A composition pattern is a proven assembly of components for a recurring communication job. Use the pattern that matches the job; let the components inside it adapt to the actual content. Communication jobs, not slide templates.

10 / Implementation

How to use the system without breaking it.

Twelve rules that keep the system honest. Most are violations of cognitive science when broken — not just preferences.

Start with the role
Before picking a component, name what each piece of information is doing. Claim, evidence, caveat, decision. Visual choices follow narrative roles, not the other way around.
Slot discipline
Required slots are required for a reason. Optional slots have a cost — every filled optional slot eats working memory (Sweller, 1988). Fill only what the story actually needs.
Schemas before novelty
A reader who's seen the same Status Ribbon shape forty times parses the forty-first without effort. Don't reinvent shapes; reuse them. Schemas are bandwidth.
Pre-attentive duty
Color and position carry meaning the eye reads first. Use Sage / Amber / Clay only as state, never as decoration. Treat color as a contract.
Encoding accuracy
For magnitude comparisons: position > length > angle > area > color saturation. Don't pie-chart what should bar-chart. Don't color-code what should position-code.
Caveat ≠ Risk
A caveat bounds. A risk warns. They use different chips and different rails. Confusing them blunts both.
Recommendation ≠ Decision
A recommendation answers. A decision asks. The Decision Gate component holds both — but the recommendation indicator must always be present and explicit.
Numbers always interpret
A bare number lies. Every Metric treatment requires a label and an interpretation slot. Trend without baseline is theatrical.
Sources earn trust
A claim without a source is opinion. Use the Verification Glyph for inline evidence; use a Source rule for the full attribution. Never claim what you can't back.
Dependency ≠ Sequence
A Dependency Connector says "X requires Y." A Hairline Bridge says "X then Y." Different concepts. Mixing them is the most common diagram failure.
First edit: removal
Tufte's data-ink rule. Every element must earn its place. Cluttered isn't depth; structured is.
If the legend is taller than the chart
It wants to be a table. The same goes for slides — if a slide needs a legend taller than its content, the slide wants to be two slides.
