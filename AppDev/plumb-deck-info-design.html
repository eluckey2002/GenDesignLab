02 / Premise

Most pages tell you what they want you to know.

The good ones let you see it.
A presentation has two jobs at once — carry information accurately, and let the audience absorb it without grinding gears. Most decks do the first and forget the second. The work that follows is six rules — Tufte's lineage, plus the perceptual research that came before and after — for closing that gap.
A page that respects the reader earns the read.

Principle 01 / Hierarchy

Hierarchy makes meaning visible.

The eye finds the important thing before the brain reads it. Your job as the author is to make sure those two events agree — that what the reader's eye lands on first is what you want them to remember.

Principle 04 / Annotation

Annotation finishes the chart.

A chart with a separate legend asks the reader to look in two places at once. Direct labels at the line's terminus put the answer where the eye is already pointing.

Principle 05 / Restraint

Restraint earns trust.

Tufte's data-ink ratio: maximize the proportion of ink that shows data over ink that decorates it. In practice, kill gridlines that don't help reading, kill borders, kill drop shadows, kill the things you put in because the slide felt empty. Empty isn't the problem. Cluttered is.
A · cluttered
B · restrained
Above all else, show the data.
Source · Tufte · The Visual Display of Quantitative Information, 1983

Principle 06 / Truth

Truth is the design's first job.

Same data, two charts. The lie sits in the y-axis: a 5-point change shown on a truncated scale (60–100) looks like a tripled outcome. Defaults aren't neutral — chart software will lie for you if you let it.

Synthesis / Six lines

Six principles, six lines.

If you took a single page of notes from this deck, it would look like the cells below. Each line is the rule; each subtitle is the cost of breaking it.
01 / Hierarchy
Make the important thing look important.
Without it, the eye and the brain disagree about what matters.
02 / Encoding
Position is the only encoding the eye reads precisely.
Pick encoding by the comparison the reader needs to make.
03 / Density
Density beats simplicity, when structured.
"One point per slide" is a workaround, not a principle.
04 / Annotation
Direct labels beat legends.
Tell the reader what matters where they're already looking.
05 / Restraint
Every element must earn its place.
First edit: removal. Second edit: more removal.
06 / Truth
The chart that lies has a short half-life.
Defaults aren't neutral. Inspect every axis you didn't pick on purpose.
